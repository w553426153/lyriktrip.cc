Attraction Overview
Yu Garden is located in the northeastern part of Shanghai’s Old City and is an outstanding representative of Jiangnan classical gardens. Originally constructed during the Jiajing and Wanli reigns of the Ming Dynasty (1559), it was built by Ming official Pan Yunduan as a filial tribute to his parents, taking the name “Yu Garden” from “Yu Yue Lao Qin” (to delight and comfort one’s parents). Covering over thirty acres, the garden has endured four centuries of change, undergone multiple rises and declines, and still retains the typical characteristics of Ming and Qing Jiangnan garden design.
The garden’s layout is ingeniously crafted using the ‘small within, vast without’ technique, creating within limited space the artistic effect of winding paths leading to seclusion and changing views with every step. Pavilions, towers, rockeries, ponds, ancient trees, and carved beams and painted rafters everywhere embody the Jiangnan garden ideal: ‘though made by man, it seems as if naturally formed.’ Yu Garden opened to the public in 1961 and was listed as a national key cultural relic protection unit by the State Council in 1982. It is the only well-preserved Ming Dynasty garden in Shanghai’s city center and serves as an important window into Jiangnan garden art and Ming-Qing architectural style.
Visit Highlights
🏔️ Great Rockery
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Shanghai_-_Yu_Garden_-_0035.jpg
Located in the northern part of the garden, this is the oldest, most exquisite, and largest yellow stone rockery in the Jiangnan region. Standing approximately 14 meters high, it was designed and constructed by the renowned Ming Dynasty rock-stacking master Zhang Nanyang, using yellow stone from Wukang, Zhejiang. The rockery features undulating peaks and intersecting gullies, presenting a majestic and imposing appearance—resembling a real mountain from afar. Ancient trees tower above, vines entwine the slopes, and winding paths lead to the summit, from which the entire garden can be overlooked. The design is ingenious: internal caves connect to form the poetic realm of ‘mountains and waters winding, doubting there is no way forward; willows darken, flowers brighten—another village appears.’ This rockery is not only the iconic landmark of Yu Garden but also a masterpiece of Chinese classical garden rock-stacking art, hailed by garden experts as the ‘Crown of Jiangnan Rockeries.’
🏛️ Wan Hua Lou
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Shanghai_-_Yu_Garden_-_0035.jpg
One of the main structures in Yu Garden, Wan Hua Lou is the best vantage point for panoramic views of the garden. This two-story pavilion features flying eaves, upturned corners, carved beams, and painted rafters, fully displaying Ming Dynasty architectural style. From the second-floor railing, one can take in the entire garden: rockeries and ponds, pavilions and towers, curved bridges and covered walkways—arranged in clear layers and harmonious disorder. In spring, the front of the building is lush with blooming flowers, hence the name ‘Wan Hua Lou’ (Ten Thousand Flowers Pavilion). The interior furnishings are simple and elegant, with plaques and couplets penned by renowned scholars, reflecting deep cultural heritage. Standing on Wan Hua Lou, one can feel the artistic charm of Jiangnan gardens: ‘one step, one scene; moving steps, changing views.’
💎 Yu Ling Long
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Shanghai_-_Yu_Garden_-_0035.jpg
Renowned as one of the "Three Great Stones of Jiangnan," this Taihu stone is the treasure of Yuyuan Garden. Standing approximately 3 meters tall, it is exquisitely perforated and translucent, embodying all four esteemed qualities of scholar’s rocks: slender, wrinkled, perforated, and translucent. The stone is densely covered with cavities; legend has it that "one hole blows smoke, seventy-two holes emit smoke." Originally a relic from the Song Dynasty’s Flower Stone Fleet, the Jade Linglong has endured a millennium of wind and rain, its texture warm and smooth like jade, with natural, flowing patterns. In the Ming Dynasty, it was acquired by Pan Yunduan and placed in the garden, becoming one of Yuyuan’s most precious landscapes. Surrounded by a pool, its reflection sways gently in the water, adding to its lively beauty. Scholars and refined gentlemen often came here to sip tea, admire the stone, compose poetry, and paint, leaving behind countless cherished anecdotes.
🌉 Nine Zigzag Bridge
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/45/Shanghai_-_Yu_Garden_-_0035.jpg
An iconic landmark connecting Yuyuan Garden and the City God Temple, this is one of Shanghai’s most famous ancient bridges. Spanning the lotus pond, the Nine Zigzag Bridge is about 30 meters long, featuring eighteen turns along its nine curves, winding with grace. The bridge deck is paved with blue stone slabs, and its railings are intricately carved, exuding ancient elegance. The bridge’s design embodies traditional Chinese cultural wisdom: first, the aesthetic principle of "a winding path leads to seclusion," enhancing the pleasure of strolling; second, according to folk legend, "evil spirits can only travel in straight lines," so the curved bridge wards off misfortune; third, it extends the walking path, allowing visitors to appreciate the garden’s beauty from multiple angles. A lakeside pavilion stands at the bridge’s center, an ideal spot for tea and rest. During festivals, the bridge throngs with visitors, while below, lotuses bloom and koi glide through the water, forming a vivid scene of a Jiangnan water town.

Restaurant Background
Founded in 1900 by Wu Xiangsheng, a pastry chef from Nanxiang Town in Jiading, Shanghai, Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant has over 120 years of history and is Shanghai’s most renowned xiaolongbao establishment. Its xiaolongbao craftsmanship is listed in Shanghai’s Intangible Cultural Heritage Register, representing the pinnacle of Shanghai-style xiaolongbao.
The term "mantou" in the restaurant’s name is a Shanghainese dialect word for baozi, a usage rooted in traditional Jiangnan customs. Nanxiang xiaolongbao is famed for its "thin skin, generous filling, abundant juice, and fresh flavor," with each bun reflecting the artisan’s mastery. The dough must be rolled to near-transparency to achieve the "skin as thin as paper" standard; the filling uses premium pork blended with secret seasonings and infused with aspic, creating the "generous filling, abundant juice" characteristic; precise control of steaming time and heat is crucial—ensuring the broth is rich without bursting the wrapper, a skill honed over years of experience.
The Yuyuan branch is the flagship store of Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant, located within Yuyuan Mall, drawing crowds daily. The restaurant features a dine-in area and a takeout section: the dine-in area offers a rustic ambiance for leisurely enjoyment, while the takeout section suits hurried visitors. No matter when you come, the place buzzes with activity, and queuing has become a ritualistic part of savoring Nanxiang xiaolongbao.
Recommended Dishes
🦀 Crab Roe and Paste Xiaolongbao (Must-try, Seasonal Recommendation)
This is the crown jewel of Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant’s offerings, available only during crab season (annually from September to January of the following year). Each crab roe and paste xiaolongbao is a handcrafted masterpiece: the wrapper is rolled to a semi-translucent state—thin as a cicada’s wing yet remarkably resilient; the filling features seasonal hairy crab roe and paste, blended with premium pork in a golden ratio, then combined with aspic. During steaming, the aspic melts, forming a rich, abundant broth.
Gently lift a crab roe and paste xiaolongbao, and through its paper-thin wrapper, you can faintly see the golden crab roe within, the broth gently swaying inside. The moment the wrapper breaks, a burst of savory crab juice erupts— the briny sweetness of crab roe, the deep richness of pork, and the fresh sweetness of the broth harmonize perfectly, creating a layered, unforgettable taste experience. The unctuousness of the crab roe intertwines with the fresh sweetness of the crab paste, leaving a lingering aftertaste that invites another bite. This dish perfectly embodies the Jiangnan culinary philosophy of "eat only what’s in season," making it an essential autumn-winter delicacy for visitors to Shanghai.
🥟 Fresh Meat Xiaolongbao (Classic Choice)
This is the classic version of Nanxiang xiaolongbao and the truest test of a chef’s skill. Though seemingly simple, it is the most demanding in technique. The filling uses premium pork foreleg, with a fat-to-lean ratio of 3:7, yielding tender, elastic meat. Seasoning is key: minced ginger removes gaminess and enhances freshness; yellow wine adds fragrance; white sugar boosts freshness; soy sauce adjusts color; and most importantly, homemade pork aspic is added—the secret to the juicy, abundant broth.
The wrapper-making process is equally meticulous: flour must be repeatedly kneaded until smooth and elastic, rested for the optimal time, and rolled to uniform thickness with thinner edges. When wrapping, each bun must feature more than 14 pleats, with the top seal shaped like a carp’s mouth for precision. During steaming, precise control of heat and timing is vital—too short, and the wrapper remains undercooked; too long, and the broth leaks out.
Steamed fresh meat xiaolongbao emerge crystal clear and glistening, the wrapper so thin it transmits light, the broth so full it trembles at the slightest touch. One bite reveals: first, the soft, tender wrapper; then, the burst of savory broth; finally, the rich, fragrant meat filling—distinct yet harmoniously united. This is the authentic xiaolongbao flavor remembered by old Shanghainese—simple, yet profoundly moving.
🥢 Eating Techniques and Etiquette
"Gently lift, slowly move, first open the window, then sip the broth, finally finish it all" — this is the standard procedure for enjoying Nanxiang xiaolongbao:
1. Gently lift: Use chopsticks to lightly grasp the pleated top of the xiaolongbao, applying minimal pressure to avoid tearing.
2. Slowly move: Gently transfer the xiaolongbao onto the spoon, keeping the motion steady to prevent broth loss.
3. First open the window: Lightly bite a small opening on the side of the xiaolongbao with your teeth to release steam and avoid burns.
4. Then sip the broth: Align the opening with your lips and gently suck out the hot, savory broth inside, savoring its fresh sweetness.
5. Finally finish it all: After sipping the broth, dip the bun lightly in vinegar and ginger threads, then place the whole xiaolongbao in your mouth and chew slowly to fully appreciate its flavor.
Regarding ingredients, the restaurant provides black vinegar and shredded young ginger. The black vinegar helps cut through greasiness and enhance freshness, but should not be used excessively, as it may overpower the delicate flavor of the soup dumplings; shredded young ginger removes fishiness and adds fragrance, making it the perfect accompaniment for crab roe soup dumplings.

Attraction Overview
Located along the Huangpu River, the Bund stretches approximately 1.5 kilometers, starting from East Yan'an Road in the south to the Waibaidu Bridge on Suzhou Creek in the north. It is Shanghai’s most iconic urban landscape, renowned as the 'Expo of International Architecture'. On the west side stand 52 buildings of diverse architectural styles, including Gothic, Romanesque, Baroque, Renaissance, and Sino-Western fusion. Constructed from the late 19th to early 20th century, these buildings once housed the financial and foreign trade institutions of old Shanghai.
The history of the Bund dates back to 1843, when Shanghai was opened as a treaty port. At that time, the area was merely a mudflat. With the establishment of foreign concessions, banks, trading houses, and consulates began constructing buildings here, gradually forming today’s international architectural complex. These buildings are not only witnesses to Shanghai’s modern history but also embody the fusion of Eastern and Western cultures, reflecting Shanghai’s transformation from a small fishing village to the 'Paris of the East'. In 1996, the Bund was designated a National Key Cultural Relic Protection Unit by the State Council, becoming a significant modern historical site and representative architectural landmark in China.
To the east lies the wide Huangpu River, where cargo ships and passenger vessels continuously pass. Opposite the Bund is the modern Lujiazui financial district, with skyscrapers such as the Oriental Pearl Tower, Jin Mao Tower, and Shanghai Tower rising into the sky, facing the historic buildings of the Bund across the river — forming the unique landscape of 'one river, two banks, a century of dialogue', perfectly embodying Shanghai’s urban spirit of 'embracing all rivers, embracing diversity'.
Architecture Highlights
🏛️ Bund No. 3 (Former British Consulate, now Huangpu Club)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/A_picture_from_China_every_day_101.jpg
Built in 1916, this is a typical British Renaissance-style building. The exterior is clad in granite, presenting a grand and dignified appearance. The façade follows a classical three-part composition: the base features rusticated stone walls symbolizing stability; the middle section is the main body with Ionic columns, elegant and luxurious; the top features a cornice with exquisite detailing. This building once served as one of Britain’s most important diplomatic institutions in the Far East, witnessing major historical events since Shanghai’s opening as a treaty port. Today, it has been converted into a high-end dining venue, with interior renovations preserving the original architectural features while incorporating modern design elements — dining here offers a perfect blend of history and modernity.
🏢 Bund No. 18 (Former Mercantile Bank of India, now AIA Building)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/A_picture_from_China_every_day_101.jpg
Constructed in 1923, this is one of the most Art Deco-style buildings in the Bund architectural complex. Standing seven stories tall, the façade features Greek Ionic columns, topped with a Baroque dome, richly ornamented and exquisite. The interior is exceptionally luxurious: the lobby is paved with imported Italian marble, the ceiling adorned with fine murals, chandeliers made of Czech crystal, and stair railings crafted from brass filigree — every detail reflects the grandeur of its era. This building once housed the headquarters of the British Mercantile Bank of India and was among the most luxurious bank buildings on the Shanghai waterfront. Today, it hosts multiple international top-tier restaurants, bars, and boutique shops, becoming one of the Bund’s most fashionable landmarks.
🏨 Peace Hotel (Former Sassoon House)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/A_picture_from_China_every_day_101.jpg
Built in 1929, this is one of the Bund’s most famous landmarks, renowned for its distinctive green copper roof. Standing 77 meters tall with 12 floors, it was among the tallest buildings in Shanghai at the time. The architectural style blends Gothic and Art Deco elements, with a façade clad in granite and Taishan-facing bricks, exuding grandeur. The building was financed by Victor Sassoon, a British-Jewish businessman, hence its alternative name, 'Sassoon House'.
Beyond being a masterpiece of architectural art, the Peace Hotel is a symbol of Shanghai’s culture. In the 1930s, it was one of the most luxurious hotels in the Far East, hosting countless international dignitaries. The hotel’s jazz bar is one of the birthplaces of Shanghai jazz music, and to this day, it maintains the tradition of live jazz performances every evening — the melodic tunes seem to transport listeners back to Shanghai’s golden era. The hotel also retains several themed suites, each decorated in the style of a different country, offering a window into the luxurious lifestyle of old Shanghai.
🕰️ Customs House (Jianghai Guan)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/A_picture_from_China_every_day_101.jpg
Built in 1927, it is one of the most recognizable buildings in the Bund architectural complex. The building stands 8 stories tall, with a clock tower reaching 13.7 meters in height at its summit. The clock tower features four large clocks, each with a diameter of 5.4 meters, making it one of the largest clocks in Asia. Manufactured in the United Kingdom, the bell chimes every 15 minutes, and on the hour plays the melody of “The East Is Red.” Its resonant and melodious sound can be heard for several kilometers, making it a “sound landmark” of the Bund.
The Customs House adopts a neoclassical architectural style, with its façade constructed of granite, exuding solemnity and grandeur. Interior decoration is equally exquisite: the lobby features marble flooring, and the ceiling is adorned with intricate reliefs. This building once served as the headquarters of China Customs, overseeing tariff collection for Shanghai and the nation, and holds an important place in modern Chinese economic history. Today, the Customs House remains in use and is among the few buildings in the Bund architectural complex that still retain their original function.

Restaurant Background
Huangpu Club is located within the historic Bund No. 3 building, originally constructed in 1916 as the British Consulate and one of the most historically significant structures in the Bund architectural complex. The restaurant masterfully blends the essence of traditional Cantonese cuisine with the distinctive charm of Hai Pai (Shanghai-style) culture, delivering a unique upscale Cantonese dining experience.
The restaurant boasts exceptional river views, with floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking the shimmering Huangpu River and the dazzling Lujiazui skyline across the water. Dining here allows guests to savor refined Cantonese dishes while enjoying the breathtaking nightscape of both banks of the Huangpu River, experiencing Shanghai’s unique “one river, two banks” allure. The interior design fuses the classical elegance of the historic building with modern minimalist aesthetics, preserving original architectural elements such as arched windows and marble columns, while integrating contemporary art installations and lighting design to create an elegant yet fashionable dining ambiance.
The head chef team at Huangpu Club hails from Guangdong, highly skilled in traditional Cantonese culinary techniques and adept at innovation, skillfully combining local ingredients with Cantonese methods to create inventive dishes that retain the essence of Cantonese cuisine while embodying Hai Pai characteristics. Ingredients are meticulously sourced: seafood is air-freighted daily from its origin to ensure freshness; roasted meats are prepared on-site by experienced masters to guarantee texture; and dim sum is handcrafted by pastry chefs, resulting in delicate and delicious bites.
Recommended Dishes
🍖 Roasted Meat Platter (Cantonese Classic, Highly Recommended)
This is one of Huangpu Club’s signature dishes, combining the three great classics of Cantonese roasted meats: char siu, roasted goose, and roasted pork. Each item is a masterpiece of craftsmanship, involving intricate and meticulous preparation techniques.
The char siu is made from premium pork neck, marinated for 24 hours in a blend of honey, soy sauce, five-spice powder, and over a dozen other seasonings to allow deep flavor penetration. Roasted using a traditional char siu oven with precise temperature and timing control, it emerges with an enticing caramelized exterior while the interior remains tender and pink. Upon slicing, the meat reveals clear grain and a balanced fat-to-lean ratio; a bite delivers a sweet, fragrant caramelized exterior giving way to tender, juicy interior — sweet with a hint of salt, salty with a touch of fragrance, offering rich layers of flavor.
The roasted goose uses Qingyuan black-feathered geese, known for their firm meat and tender skin. Prior to roasting, the goose is inflated to separate skin from meat, then coated with a proprietary sauce and roasted in a hanging oven. The finished product features a glossy, golden-red skin that shimmers with oil, as crisp as paper — a light touch produces a satisfying “crack.” The meat is succulent and juicy, fatty yet not greasy, carrying a subtle aroma of fruitwood. Served with a specially prepared plum sauce, the sweet-and-sour condiment cuts through the richness, enhancing the overall flavor.
Roast pork is the pinnacle of kung fu dishes in Cantonese cuisine, testing the chef’s mastery of heat control. Using pork belly, the skin must be roasted until crisp like glass, shattering at the first bite; the meat must remain tender and juicy, with the fat melting in the mouth and the lean portion firm and chewy. This dual texture of crispy skin and tender meat is the ultimate achievement in roast pork.
🦞 Steamed Seafood (Seasonal Recommendation, Ultimate Freshness)
Cantonese cuisine emphasizes ‘eat only what’s in season,’ and the seafood at Huangpu Club is always the freshest, most plump seasonal varieties, air-flown daily from the source. Steaming is the best way to showcase the natural flavor of seafood and also the most demanding test of ingredient freshness.
Steamed grouper is one of the signature dishes, using wild grouper with snow-white, delicate flesh and clear texture. Only a few ginger shreds and scallion sections are added during steaming to remove fishiness; after steaming, it is drizzled with scorching peanut oil and soy sauce to awaken the fish’s fragrance. The fish melts in the mouth, incredibly sweet and fresh, with no trace of fishiness—pure oceanic purity.
Seasonal sea shrimp, flower crab, and geoduck are also must-tries, each retaining its most authentic freshness, allowing diners to taste the ocean’s bounty.
🥟 Cantonese Dim Sum (Delicate Snacks, Tea Time Favorite)
The dim sum kitchen at Huangpu Club is led by experienced dim sum chefs, with every item handcrafted, exquisite and delicious.
Shrimp dumplings are the hallmark of Cantonese dim sum—skin as thin as paper, crystal clear and translucent, allowing the pink shrimp filling inside to be clearly visible. The wrapper is made from wheat starch (clear flour), becoming semi-transparent when steamed, with a soft, smooth texture. The filling uses fresh shrimp, with at least three shrimp per dumpling, complemented by a few diced bamboo shoots for texture. One bite yields crisp, springy, juicy, sweet shrimp—an essential order for Cantonese morning tea.
Shumai is another classic: pork filling with shiitake mushrooms, water chestnuts, and other ingredients, wrapped in thin dough, topped with golden crab roe or shrimp roe, visually tempting. After steaming, the wrapper is soft and glutinous, the filling fragrant, the broth abundant—one bite fills the mouth with aroma.

Project Introduction
The Huangpu River cruise is Shanghai’s iconic waterfront sightseeing project, hailed as the ‘First Cruise on the Huangpu,’ and one of the best ways to experience the charm of Shanghai’s ‘Sleepless City.’ The Huangpu River is Shanghai’s mother river, stretching 113 kilometers, flowing through the urban area and dividing the city into Puxi and Pudong. The cruise route is meticulously designed, linking the Bund’s World Architecture Expo, Lujiazui’s skyscraper cluster, Nanpu Bridge, Yangpu Bridge, and other Shanghai landmarks, allowing visitors to witness the essence of Shanghai’s century-long history and modern development in just over an hour.
Nighttime is the ideal moment to take the cruise. As night falls and lights come on, both banks of the Huangpu River instantly transform into a stage of light and shadow. The Bund’s historic buildings, bathed in golden light, reveal a century of weathered elegance; Lujiazui’s skyscrapers dazzle with modern LED light shows, showcasing technology and the future. The Oriental Pearl’s colorful spheres shimmer and shift in the night sky, the Shanghai Tower’s spire pierces the clouds, and the Jin Mao Tower’s ‘bottle opener’ silhouette stands out strikingly under the lights. The cruise glides slowly across the river, river breeze brushing the face, banks ablaze with lights, their reflections shimmering on the water—showcasing the unique allure of Shanghai’s ‘Sleepless City.’
Professional commentators on board provide bilingual (Chinese and English) explanations of the history and stories behind the sights, allowing guests to deeply understand Shanghai’s history and culture while enjoying the scenery. Some luxury cruises also offer dining services, enabling guests to enjoy dinner while sightseeing—a uniquely charming experience.

Attraction Overview
Zhujiacao Ancient Town is located in Qingpu District, Shanghai, and is one of Shanghai's four major historical and cultural towns, with a history of over 1,700 years, renowned as the 'Venice of Shanghai.' Situated by Dian Shan Lake, the town is built along the water, with crisscrossing rivers forming the typical Jiangnan water town layout of 'small bridges, flowing water, and homes.' During the Ming and Qing dynasties, Zhujiacao became an important commercial hub in Jiangnan due to prosperous water transport, with 'a three-li-long street lined with a thousand shops,' enjoying a brief period of prosperity.
The ancient town preserves hundreds of well-preserved Ming and Qing dynasty buildings, featuring gray bricks and dark tiles, white walls and dark tiles, and horse-head walls staggered in height, showcasing the typical architectural style of Jiangnan. Thirty-six ancient bridges span the rivers, the most famous being the Fangsheng Bridge, built during the Wanli period of the Ming Dynasty (1571), which is the largest, highest, and longest five-arch stone bridge in Shanghai, measuring 70.8 meters in length, 5.8 meters in width, and 7.4 meters in height, grand and imposing.
Strolling along the ancient town's blue stone slab roads, on both sides are white-walled, dark-tiled Jiangnan residences, with red lanterns hanging at the doors and exquisitely carved window lattices. In the canals, punt boats glide slowly, boatwomen humming soft Wu dialect melodies, the oars striking the water with rhythmic 'creak' sounds, as if transported back to the Jiangnan water towns of the Ming and Qing eras. The town retains numerous historical relics such as the City God Temple, Kezhi Garden, Qing Dynasty Post Office, and Humanities and Art Museum, each bearing profound historical and cultural significance.

🍚 Grandma’s Zongzi (Ancient Town Specialty, Traditional Flavor)
Zhujiajiao’s Grandma’s Zongzi is the most representative traditional snack of the ancient town, handmade by local elderly women, inheriting a craft passed down for hundreds of years. 🥩 Braised Pork (Traditional Jiangnan dish, the taste of home)
Braised pork is a traditional famous dish in the Jiangnan region, and Zhujiajiao’s version is especially renowned. The name comes from the preparation method: red-braised pork belly is wrapped in zongzi or lotus leaves and tied tightly, then steamed in a basket. This allows the meat to fully absorb the leaf’s fragrance while maintaining its tender texture and rich broth.
Ingredient selection is meticulous: must be skin-on pork belly with balanced fat and lean, in proper proportion. The meat is cut into squares, marinated with soy sauce, yellow wine, rock sugar, star anise, cinnamon, etc., then red-braised until tender. Controlling the heat during braising is key—use low heat and slow simmering to let the meat fully absorb the seasonings while keeping its shape intact. The cooked meat is glossy and oily, then wrapped in leaves and steamed, allowing the leaf’s fragrance to penetrate, cutting greasiness and enhancing aroma.
Unwrapping the leaves releases a rich blend of meat and leaf fragrance. The meat skin is soft and chewy, the fat melts in the mouth, the lean is tender without being dry, and the broth is thick—typical of Jiangnan’s rich, oily, red-braised flavor. This dish is fatty but not greasy, sweet with a hint of salt, a must-have hard dish for Jiangnan families during festivals, and a taste of home in the memories of those far away.
🐟 Steamed Fish (Specialty of Dianshan Lake, extremely tender)
White water fish is a specialty of Dianshan Lake, scientifically known as ‘Habronotus,’ named for its snow-white, tender flesh. This fish grows in the clear waters of Dianshan Lake, feeding on small fish and shrimp, giving it exceptionally fresh flavor without any muddy taste, making it one of the most popular freshwater fish in Jiangnan.
In Zhujiajiao, the preparation emphasizes preserving the original flavor, most commonly steamed or braised. Steamed white water fish only adds ginger strips and scallion sections to remove fishiness; after steaming, it’s drizzled with hot oil and soy sauce. The fish meat is snow-white like jade, tender like tofu, flaking apart at a light touch, melting in the mouth with exquisite sweetness—pure lake freshness. Braised white water fish adds soy sauce, yellow wine, rock sugar, etc.; after cooking, the fish absorbs the sauce’s fragrance, turns reddish-brown, and achieves a balanced salty-sweet flavor with a unique character.
🫘 Smoked Green Beans (Drinking snack, more fragrant the more you chew)
Smoked green beans are a traditional snack from Zhujiajiao and one of the most popular drinking snacks in Jiangnan. Made from locally grown green beans, they undergo selection, washing, boiling, and smoking. The beans are first boiled to half-cooked, then placed in a smoker using natural materials like tea leaves, rice husks, and orange peel to absorb smoky aroma while retaining crunch.
The smoked beans turn dark brown, surface slightly wrinkled, emitting a unique smoky fragrance. Eating them: outer shell crisp, inside soft and glutinous, pleasantly salty and fragrant—more chewing, more aroma. They can be enjoyed as a snack or with drinks; paired with yellow wine or beer, chatting while eating, they are part of Jiangnan’s leisure life. Smoked green beans also make great gifts—vacuum-packed, they can be stored for months, letting friends and family taste the specialty flavors of Jiangnan’s water towns.

Attraction Overview
Xintiandi, based on Shanghai’s unique shikumen architecture, has been transformed into a fashionable leisure pedestrian street integrating dining, commerce, entertainment, and culture—a successful example of Shanghai’s urban renewal. Developed by Hong Kong’s Swire Group, it officially opened in 2001, pioneering ‘protective development’ by preserving the full appearance of traditional shikumen laneways while injecting modern elements inside, becoming one of Shanghai’s most distinctive urban tourism attractions.
Shikumen architecture is a unique residential form in Shanghai, originating in the mid-19th century as a product of Chinese-Western cultural collision and fusion. The term ‘shikumen’ comes from its signature door frame: made of stone strips or blocks, with heavy black-lacquered wooden doors, and lintels carved with exquisite brick or stone patterns, commonly featuring traditional auspicious motifs like ‘Fu Lu Shou Xi’ (blessing, prosperity, longevity, happiness) or ‘Ji Xiang Ru Yi’ (auspicious as desired). The buildings combine Jiangnan traditional courtyard and wing-room layouts with Western terraced house appearance and structure, forming a unique ‘East-West fusion’ style.
Shikumen complexes were once Shanghai’s dominant housing form, accounting for over 60% of urban residential area at their peak. They hold the collective memory of generations of Shanghainese: narrow alleys where neighbors looked out for each other; courtyards where children played; in front of shikumen doors, residents sat on small stools to cool off and chat. This ‘seventy-two tenants’ lifestyle fostered a unique Hai派 culture and neighborly relations.
Xintiandi’s renovation preserved the appearance and spatial layout of shikumen buildings—gray brick walls, black-lacquered doors, shikumen door frames, carved window lattices—all kept intact, while the interior underwent modernization, introducing international brand restaurants, bars, boutiques, and art galleries. By day, it’s a fashionable shopping and leisure spot; at night, lights blaze, bars, restaurants, and boutiques glow, outdoor seats filled with people sipping wine and chatting, live music plays, showcasing the charming allure of modern Shanghai.

🥩 Braised Pork Belly (The Soul of Huaiyang Cuisine, a Must-Order Classic)
Braised pork belly is the representative dish of Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine and the best embodiment of the 'rich oil and red sauce' cooking style. The braised pork belly at Shanghai Bund is exceptional—every step, from ingredient selection to cooking, is pursued with perfection.
The pork must be skin-on five-spice pork with a golden ratio of fat to lean (40% fat, 60% lean), so the resulting meat is fatty but not greasy, lean but not dry. The meat is cut into 4-centimeter cubes, maintaining intact shape. Before cooking, the meat cubes are blanched to remove blood foam, then seared in the pan until the surface turns slightly yellow to lock in juices.
The braising process is key: first sauté scallions and ginger to release fragrance, add cooking wine to remove fishiness and enhance aroma, then add soy sauce, rock sugar, star anise, cinnamon bark, and other seasonings, pour in broth, bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to low heat and simmer slowly. Simmering time must be at least 2 hours to allow the meat to fully absorb the seasonings’ aroma while maintaining its shape. During this time, constantly turn the meat to ensure even coloring and flavor absorption. Finally, increase heat to reduce the sauce until thick and glossy, tightly coating the meat pieces.
The finished braised pork belly shines amber-red and glossy, emitting rich sauce and meat aromas. Pick up a piece—the skin is soft, tender, and Q-bouncy, melting instantly upon a light bite; the fatty part melts in the mouth with no greasiness, only rich sweetness; the lean part is tender yet not dry, with distinct fibers and abundant meat flavor. 🐟 Pan-Fried Belt Fish (Shanghainese Kung Fu Dish, Crispy Outside, Tender Inside)
Pan-fried belt fish is a common dish on Shanghainese tables—seemingly simple, yet it truly tests the chef’s skill. Shanghai Bund uses wild-caught belt fish from the East China Sea, which has delicate texture and rich flavor, far superior to farmed belt fish.
After cleaning, the belt fish is cut into sections and marinated with salt, cooking wine, and ginger juice to remove fishiness. Marination time must be just right—too short and it won’t absorb flavor; too long and the meat becomes tough. Before frying, lightly coat the fish with a thin layer of dry starch—this is the secret to keeping the fish meat tender.
When frying, the oil must be hot. Once in the pan, do not rush to flip—let the bottom side fry thoroughly until golden and crispy, forming a protective layer that locks in internal moisture and freshness. After flipping, fry the other side until equally golden, achieving the 'crispy outside, tender inside' effect on both sides. After frying, drizzle with prepared sauce made from soy sauce, vinegar, sugar, ginger mince, and garlic mince—sour, sweet, salty, fresh, and fragrant.
The finished pan-fried belt fish is golden yellow, with a crispy skin that makes a 'crack' sound when lightly touched; the inside is snow-white and tender, retaining its original freshness with no trace of fishiness. Dip in the sauce—the sour-sweet sauce cuts through the fish’s richness, making it more appetizing and perfect with rice. This dish combines the freshness of seafood with the crispiness of frying, a home-style favorite Shanghainese never tire of.

🦀 Crab Roe (Seasonal Seafood, Autumn-Winter Delicacy)
Crab roe is a seasonal delicacy in autumn and winter—at this time, crabs are at their fattiest, with full crab roe and abundant crab fat. Shanghai Bund selects Taihu hairy crabs or Chongming clear-water crabs, both renowned Jiangnan crabs known for sweet, tender meat and rich, abundant roe.
The primary cooking method is steaming, to maximally preserve the crab’s natural flavor. Opening the shell reveals golden-yellow crab roe like congealed fat—melting in the mouth, fine and delicate, richly fragrant; the crab fat is crystal-clear and translucent, with a soft, smooth texture and incomparable sweetness; the crab meat is snow-white and plump, with distinct strands, tender and juicy.

Shanghainese follow the tradition of 'nine females, ten males'—eat female crabs in the ninth lunar month (when roe is full), male crabs in the tenth month (when fat is abundant)—a dietary wisdom accumulated over hundreds of years.

Recommended Dishes
🐟 Steamed Grouper (Cantonese Classic, Supreme Freshness)
Steamed grouper is a representative dish of Cantonese seafood cuisine and the best showcase of seafood freshness and chef skill. Guoji Seafood uses wild grouper, which grows in deep seas, featuring exceptionally tender meat and high nutritional value, earning it the reputation of 'sea bass'.
When preparing the grouper, the fish must be kept intact—scales, gills, and internal organs removed, then cleaned thoroughly. A few slits are made on each side of the fish to aid flavor absorption and even steaming. During steaming, only a small amount of ginger strips and scallion segments are added to remove fishiness, with no other seasonings used, relying entirely on the fish’s natural freshness. Steaming time must be precisely controlled: generally, 8–10 minutes per 500 grams of fish; too short results in undercooking, too long makes the meat tough.
After steaming, remove the ginger and scallions, replace with fresh scallion strips, drizzle with sizzling peanut oil to release the scallion aroma, then pour over specially prepared soy sauce (a seasoned soy sauce)—this is the standard method for Cantonese steamed seafood. The steamed grouper emerges with snow-white, jade-like flesh, clear texture; a gentle nudge with chopsticks causes the flesh to flake apart in pieces, melting in the mouth. 🦐 Poached Shrimp (Simplicity at Its Peak, Freshness at Its Peak)
Poaching is one of the most classic cooking methods in Cantonese cuisine, named 'white poaching' because it involves boiling in plain water without any seasonings, fully preserving the ingredient’s original flavor. Guoji Seafood’s white-poached shrimp uses shrimp caught the same day—transparent bodies, intact antennae, lively and jumping, indicating top-tier freshness.
During poaching, only ginger slices and cooking wine are added to the water to remove fishiness. Once the water boils, the shrimp are added and cooked until their bodies turn red and flesh turns white, then immediately removed—the entire process takes no more than 3 minutes. Precise timing is crucial: overcooking makes the shrimp meat tough and loses its springy texture. After removal, the shrimp are immediately plunged into ice water to rapidly cool the meat, preserving its crisp texture.
When served, the shrimp display a vibrant orange-red hue, shiny shells, and upright antennae. Peeling back the shell reveals crystal-clear, firm, and plump meat. Dipped in a specially prepared sauce (minced ginger, minced scallion, soy sauce, sesame oil), one bite delivers shrimp meat that is springy, crisp, juicy, and bursting with the pure, fresh taste of the sea—irresistible. It uses imported Italian Carnaroli or Arborio rice, which have high starch content, allowing the grains to retain their integrity and achieve a creamy texture when cooked. The process begins by sautéing butter and onions, then adding the rice and stirring until translucent. The entire process takes about 20 minutes, and the final texture should be ‘al dente’—tender on the outside, firm in the center—the standard for authentic Italian risotto.
Before heating the pot, add butter, Parmesan cheese, and truffle slices, stir evenly, let the cheese melt, and fully blend with the rice grains. After plating, shave fresh truffle slices onto the surface of the risotto; the aroma of truffle is instantly released by the heat, filling the air. 🥩 Low-Temperature Lamb (Modern Technique, Tender and Flavorful)
Low-temperature lamb is a creative dish at Tavola, using modern molecular gastronomy’s sous-vide low-temperature slow-cooking technique to maximize the tenderness and juiciness of lamb. It uses imported New Zealand lamb, known for its fine texture and lack of gaminess, making it ideal for premium cuisine.

This long, low-temperature cooking allows proteins to denature slowly, resulting in exceptionally tender meat while retaining internal moisture and nutrients — unlike traditional methods that cause juice loss.
After slow-cooking, the lamb surface is quickly seared at high heat to form a fragrant crust, locking in juices and triggering the Maillard reaction to enhance aroma and texture. Attraction Overview
Wukang Road was originally named Ferguson Road (Route Ferguson), established in 1907 and named after American missionary and scholar John Calvin Ferguson. This 1.17-kilometer-long, 12-meter-wide road is one of Shanghai’s most distinctive historical and cultural districts, acclaimed as the “condensed century of Shanghai’s modern history” and known as the “Celebrity Road.” In 2011, Wukang Road was selected as a “China Historical and Cultural Famous Street,” becoming an important emblem of Shanghai’s urban culture.
Wukang Road is located in Xuhui District, Shanghai, and was once a high-end residential area in the French Concession. The roadsides are lined with plane trees, most over a century old. These French plane trees were planted by the French in the early 20th century and have now grown into towering trees with lush foliage, forming a green tunnel. In spring and summer, sunlight filters through the leaves, casting dappled shadows on the ground; in autumn, golden leaves blanket the pavement, rustling underfoot with romantic charm; in winter, the bare branches outline elegant silhouettes, offering a unique serenity.
This road preserves numerous historical buildings, including Spanish-style, French Renaissance-style, and English country villa-style old mansions, totaling 37 outstanding historical buildings, 14 of which are Shanghai municipal cultural relics protection units. Most of these buildings were constructed in the 1920s and 1930s and once housed foreign expatriates, Republican-era officials, and cultural celebrities. Literary giant Ba Jin, revolutionary Huang Xing, Song Qingling, Guo Moruo, Ke Ling, and many other historical figures once resided here, each building carrying a legendary story.
Today, Wukang Road preserves its historical architecture while injecting new cultural vitality. Free from the noise of commercial streets, it maintains a tranquil and elegant atmosphere, making it one of Shanghai’s best streets for strolling. 🐍 Xiangyou Eel Strips (Gongfu Dish, Fragrant and Aromatic)
Xiangyou Eel Strips is a signature Shanghainese gongfu dish, named for the sizzling sound (‘zizila’) made when hot oil is poured over it at the end—a hallmark of its preparation. This dish demands exceptional ingredient quality and knife skills, serving as a true test of a chef’s mastery.
The eel must be fresh, live yellow eel to ensure tender, delicate flesh. Preparing yellow eel is a technical process: bones, entrails, and blood must be removed, leaving only the pure meat, which is then cut into fine, uniform strips—about the thickness of a matchstick. The strips are repeatedly rinsed in water to remove slime and fishy odor, then marinated with cooking wine, salt, and white pepper to infuse flavor.
Heat control is critical during stir-frying: oil must be hot, and the eel strips should be quickly tossed in the wok for no more than one minute—overcooking makes the meat tough. After stir-frying, the eel is plated, generously topped with scallion and ginger threads, then drenched with sizzling hot oil. The ‘zizila’ sound erupts as the aromatics release their fragrance instantly, blending with the eel’s natural freshness into an intoxicating, nose-tingling aroma—this is the essence of ‘xiangyou’ (fragrant oil).
When tasted, the eel strips are silky, smooth, and refreshing, with zero fishiness—only pure, clean freshness. The pungent scallion-ginger cuts through any gaminess and enhances freshness, while the hot oil adds a deep, toasty layer of complexity. Poached chicken is a Shanghai classic snack and the ultimate expression of ingredient purity. ‘Poached’ means cooked in plain water without any added seasonings—relying entirely on the chicken’s natural freshness. Attraction Overview
Jing’an Temple is a renowned thousand-year-old Buddhist temple in Shanghai, originally built in the 10th year of Chiwu during the Eastern Wu period of the Three Kingdoms (247 AD), making it over 1,700 years old—one of the oldest Buddhist temples in Shanghai and among the oldest in southern China. The name 'Jing’an' is derived from the Buddhist concept of 'serene contemplation and mindfulness of breathing,' meaning inner peace achieved through meditation.
The history of Jing’an Temple dates back to the Three Kingdoms period, when it was originally named 'Hudu Chongyuan Temple' and located beside the Wusong River (today’s Suzhou Creek). During the Song Dynasty, it was relocated to its current site and renamed Jing’an Temple. The temple has undergone repeated cycles of rise and fall through dynastic changes, yet its incense has never ceased. During the Ming and Qing dynasties, Jing’an Temple was one of the Buddhist centers of the Jiangnan region, producing many eminent monks and attracting countless devotees. In the Republican era, the area around Jing’an Temple gradually developed into Shanghai’s commercial hub, creating the unique landscape of 'a pure land amidst the bustling city'.
The current temple complex was rebuilt in the 2000s, covering approximately 22,000 square meters with a building area of about 20,000 square meters. The architectural style blends features of Tibetan and Han Buddhism: it retains the traditional layout of Han Buddhist temples while incorporating decorative elements from Tibetan Buddhist architecture, forming a distinctive style. The entire complex is resplendent with gold and grandeur, featuring key structures such as the Mountain Gate, Hall of the Heavenly Kings, Mahavira Hall, Hall of the Three Saints, and the Abbot’s Room, arranged sequentially along the central axis.
Located in Shanghai’s most prosperous central district, Jing’an Temple is surrounded by towering commercial skyscrapers, with a metro station right at its entrance—constant traffic and flowing crowds. Yet, once you step through the temple gates, the noise is instantly shut out, replaced only by the gentle echo of sutras and curling incense smoke, bringing peace to the mind. This 'great seclusion in the city' layout embodies the Buddhist ideal of 'engaging with the world without being tainted by it,' and has become a spiritual sanctuary for urban dwellers seeking solace.
At nightfall, Jing’an Temple’s night view becomes especially enchanting. The temple’s lighting system has been meticulously designed: golden light illuminates the roofs, pillars, and upturned eaves, making the entire temple shimmer like a golden palace against the night sky. Compared to the neon lights of surrounding modern buildings, Jing’an Temple’s glow appears solemn and dignified yet retains its splendor, showcasing the harmonious coexistence of tradition and modernity.

Recommended Dishes
🐟 Shanghai Smoked Fish (Huaiyang signature dish, crispy and fragrant)
Shanghai Smoked Fish is a classic cold appetizer in Huaiyang cuisine and a必备 dish at Shanghai banquets. Despite its name suggesting smoking, it is actually made by deep-frying fish and then soaking it in brine; the name comes from its resemblance to smoked fish in color and texture. Fu 1088's version uses wild East China Sea grass carp or pomfret—tender, boneless fish ideal for this dish.
After cleaning, the fish is sliced into approximately 1 cm thick pieces, marinated with salt, yellow wine, and ginger juice to remove fishiness and infuse flavor. It is then lightly coated with starch and deep-fried until golden and crisp—a crucial step for achieving the 'crispy exterior' texture. Oil temperature must be high, and timing precise: fry until the outside is crisp and the inside just cooked through.
The brine is the soul of the dish, made by simmering over a dozen ingredients including soy sauce, yellow wine, rock sugar, star anise, cassia bark, dried tangerine peel, and cloves until thick and fragrant. Hot fried fish slices are immediately immersed in the brine to absorb flavor, soaked for about 30 minutes. After removal, they are chilled in the refrigerator, allowing the brine to solidify on the surface into a glossy 'sugar coating'.
When chilled, the smoked fish displays a bright red hue with a glossy sheen, emitting rich soy and spice aromas. A bite yields a crisp exterior with a satisfying 'crack', while the interior remains tender and juicy, preserving its natural freshness. The brine's savoriness, rock sugar's sweetness, and spices' fragrance unfold in layers, creating a rich yet harmonious mouthfeel. Even the fried fish bones become crisp and edible—calcium-rich and delicious. Best served cold, it pairs excellently with alcohol and stands as a classic cold platter at banquets.
🐍 Sizzling Oil Eel Strips (Kung Fu dish, intensely aromatic)
Sizzling Oil Eel Strips is one of Fu 1088's signature dishes and a quintessential Kung Fu dish in Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine, demanding exceptional ingredient quality, knife skills, and heat control—a true test of a chef's comprehensive skill.
Fresh live eels weighing 250–300 grams are ideal, as their meat is most tender at this size. Eel preparation is meticulous: bones, entrails, blood, and slime must be removed, leaving only the meat—requiring skilled knife work and experience. The cleaned eel meat is cut into uniform strips, about the thickness of a matchstick and roughly 10 cm long, for optimal texture when stir-fried.
After cutting, the eel strips are repeatedly rinsed in water to remove residual slime and fishiness, then marinated with cooking wine, salt, white pepper, and ginger juice for about 15 minutes. Stir-frying hinges on heat: the wok must be smoking hot, oil at about 80% heat, and the eel strips added and quickly stir-fried, seasoned with soy sauce, yellow wine, and white sugar—entire process must not exceed one minute, or the strips will turn tough and chewy.
Immediately after cooking, the dish is plated, topped with generous amounts of scallion and ginger strips, followed by the critical step: pouring hot oil. The oil must be smoking hot; when poured, it produces a loud 'hiss', instantly releasing the fragrance of scallion and ginger, which blends with the eel's freshness to create an intense, layered aroma—this is the origin of 'sizzling oil' and the dish's most thrilling moment.
Best enjoyed hot, the eel strips are tender, smooth, and melt-in-the-mouth, with no fishiness—only pure freshness. The pungency of scallion and ginger cuts through fishiness and enhances freshness, while the charred aroma from hot oil adds depth. The savory sauce, hint of sweetness from white sugar, and mellow fragrance of yellow wine build in layers. This dish pairs exceptionally well with rice—eel strips' juices mixed with rice are utterly delicious, often prompting an extra bowl.
🧈 Crab Roe Tofu (Jiangnan delicacy, fresh and silky)
Crab Roe Tofu is a traditional Jiangnan delicacy and a representative dish of Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine, perfectly combining the silkiness of tofu with the fresh fragrance of crab roe, offering rich texture and high nutritional value. Fu 1088's version uses premium ingredients and meticulous preparation, making it a must-order item.
The tofu used is soft tofu (southern tofu), high in water content and delicate in texture like fat, melting in the mouth. It is cut into small pieces, blanched in boiling water to remove beany odor while preserving shape. Crab roe and meat are sourced from seasonal hairy crabs, the source of the dish's freshness.
Cooking begins with sautéing minced ginger until fragrant, then stir-frying in the crab roe, which releases golden crab oil when heated, filling the air with aroma. Stock, yellow wine, salt, and white pepper are added, followed by tofu cubes, gently stirred to ensure even heating and absorption of broth. Heat must be low and movements gentle to avoid breaking the tofu. Finally, the sauce is thickened with cornstarch to cling tightly to the tofu, garnished with chopped scallions.
The finished dish presents a golden hue, thick and fragrant sauce. A spoonful delivers tofu that melts like fat, while the crab roe bursts with rich, oily sweetness from the roe and freshness from the meat; the broth is savory and mellow, with a hint of yellow wine aroma. The texture is delicate—fresh without fishiness, smooth without greasiness—a delight enjoyed by all ages.
🥬 Blanch-ed Chrysanthemum Greens Rings (Time-honored classic, grassy fragrance, delicious meat)
Grass head circle is a classic dish in Shanghai Benbang cuisine. “Grass head” is the Shanghainese term for alfalfa (also known as Malan head), and “circle” refers to pork large intestine. This dish combines the fresh fragrance of grass head with the rich richness of pork large intestine, creating a unique flavor that is a home-style delicacy remembered by old Shanghainese.
The key to preparing pork large intestine lies in repeated washing to remove odor, then marinating with cooking wine, ginger juice, and salt, followed by blanching to remove fishiness. After treatment, the intestine is cut into rings and braised in a pot with soy sauce, yellow wine, rock sugar, star anise, and other seasonings, simmered over low heat until tender and flavorful.
Grass head should be selected as fresh tender shoots, washed clean, then quickly stir-fried over high heat, seasoned with salt and minced garlic, cooked until just done to retain its vibrant green color and fresh fragrance. Finally, the braised pork intestine rings are mixed with the stir-fried grass head and tossed evenly, allowing the grass head’s fragrance to penetrate the intestine, while the intestine’s richness envelops the grass head.
The brilliance of this dish lies in the fusion of grass fragrance and meat aroma: the pork intestine is soft, glutinous, and rich, carrying the salty freshness of rich oil and red sauce; the grass head is refreshing, crisp, and tender, carrying the fragrance of plants. Together, they balance meat and vegetable, cutting through the greasiness of the intestine while enhancing the freshness of the grass head, resulting in rich texture layers and balanced nutrition. This dish is a classic at Shanghai’s time-honored restaurants, carrying the culinary memories of generations of Shanghainese.

Attraction Overview
Founded in 1952, the Shanghai Museum is a large museum of ancient Chinese art and one of China’s most important museums. Located south of People’s Square, it enjoys a superior geographical location and convenient transportation. The new building was completed and opened in 1996, with a floor area of 39,200 square meters, a height of 29.5 meters, five above-ground floors and two underground floors, housing nearly one million cultural relics, of which 120,000 are fine pieces, especially renowned for bronzes, ceramics, calligraphy, and paintings, enjoying high reputation both domestically and internationally.
The museum’s architecture was designed by renowned architect Tonghe Xing, with a unique form and profound meaning. The overall design features a circular roof and square base, symbolizing the traditional Chinese cosmological concept of ‘heaven is round, earth is square.’ The circular roof uses glass and steel structure, like a giant mirror reflecting the sky and surrounding buildings; the square base is constructed of granite, solemn and majestic. This design is not only aesthetically pleasing but also embodies the traditional Chinese philosophy of ‘harmony between heaven and humanity,’ serving as a model of perfect integration between modern architecture and traditional culture.
The museum has 11 specialized exhibition halls and 3 exhibition galleries, displaying the essence of ancient Chinese art. Exhibits are arranged according to historical development and artistic categories, systematically showcasing the glorious achievements of China’s five-thousand-year civilization. The artifacts are not only numerous but also of exceptional quality, many being national treasures, such as the Da Ke Ding from the Shang Dynasty, the Mao Gong Ding from the Western Zhou, Tang Dynasty blue-and-white porcelain, and Song Dynasty calligraphy and paintings—treasures among Chinese cultural relics.
The museum employs modern exhibition methods, combining traditional display with multimedia, interactive installations, and virtual reality technology to bring artifacts to life and make history dynamic. Each exhibition hall features detailed Chinese and English explanations, with some artifacts offering audio guides to help visitors deeply understand the history and culture behind the relics. The museum regularly hosts temporary exhibitions and academic lectures, serving as an important window for understanding traditional Chinese culture and enhancing artistic cultivation.

🏺 Bronze Ware Hall (Treasure of the Museum, National Heavy Weapon)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/20694-Shanghai_%2832225501024%29.jpg
The Bronze Ware Hall is one of the most important exhibition halls in the Shanghai Museum, housing over 400 pieces of ancient Chinese bronzes spanning from the Xia and Shang dynasties to the Qin and Han dynasties, systematically presenting the development of Chinese bronze ware. The quality and quantity of its collection rank among the top in domestic museums.
The museum’s treasures include replicas of the Da Ke Ding and Mao Gong Ding (the originals are respectively stored in the Shanghai Museum’s storage and the Taipei Palace Museum). The Da Ke Ding is a late Western Zhou bronze vessel, 93.1 cm in height and weighing 201.5 kg, with a majestic form and exquisite decoration. Its inner wall bears a 290-character inscription recording important historical events of the Western Zhou period, making it a crucial artifact for studying Western Zhou history.
The exhibition hall displays bronzes according to their functions: vessels for food (ding, gui, dou, etc.), vessels for wine (jue, gu, zhi, etc.), vessels for water (pan, yi, etc.), musical instruments (bianzhong, zheng, etc.), and weapons (ge, mao, jian, etc.). Each category includes detailed descriptions explaining their uses, manufacturing techniques, and symbolic meanings of the patterns.
Of particular note are the decorative motifs on the bronzes, including taotie patterns, kui dragon patterns, and phoenix-bird patterns. These designs are not only ornamental but also carry profound cultural and religious significance. By observing the evolution of these motifs, one can understand the development of ancient Chinese society and changes in aesthetic concepts. The exhibition also showcases bronze casting techniques, such as the piece-mold process and lost-wax method, allowing visitors to appreciate the wisdom and skill of ancient artisans.
🏺 Ceramics Gallery (The Beauty of Porcelain, A Thousand-Year Legacy)
Image: https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/20694-Shanghai_%2832225501024%29.jpg
The Ceramics Gallery houses over 500 exquisite Chinese ceramic pieces spanning from Neolithic painted pottery to Qing dynasty famille rose porcelain, covering nearly ten thousand years and systematically presenting the development of Chinese ceramic art. China is the birthplace of porcelain, and the word 'China' itself carries the meaning of 'porcelain,' reflecting the global stature of Chinese ceramics.
The exhibition is organized by historical dynasty and kiln type. Neolithic painted pottery features vibrant colors and simple patterns, reflecting the aesthetic sensibilities of early inhabitants; Shang and Zhou dynasty proto-porcelain marked the beginning of Chinese porcelain; Tang dynasty sancai glazed ceramics are vivid in color and lively in form; Song dynasty celadon, white porcelain, and black porcelain each possess distinct characteristics, embodying the Song aesthetic of 'simplicity and elegance'; Yuan dynasty blue-and-white porcelain pioneered underglaze decoration; Ming and Qing dynasty colored porcelain reached the pinnacle of Chinese ceramic artistry.
Special attention should be given to the works of the Five Great Kilns: Ru ware’s celestial blue, Guan ware’s ice-crackle pattern, Ge ware’s golden threads and iron lines, Jun ware’s transmutation glaze, and Ding ware’s incised decoration—each a masterpiece of ceramic art. Additionally, Jingdezhen’s blue-and-white and famille rose porcelain, Longquan’s celadon, and Dehua’s blanc de Chine showcase the unique styles of China’s major kilns.
The gallery also features an interactive display area using multimedia technology to demonstrate the porcelain-making process, including material selection, throwing, trimming, glazing, and firing, enabling visitors to understand how a piece of porcelain transforms from clay into a work of art.

The Calligraphy and Painting Gallery houses masterpieces by renowned artists from the Jin, Tang, Song, Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties. The calligraphy section traces the evolution of Chinese script: from oracle bone script and bronze inscriptions to seal script, clerical script, regular script, running script, and cursive script, each represented by exemplary works. Masterpieces by Wang Xizhi, Yan Zhenqing, Su Dongpo, Mi Fu, and Zhao Mengfu reveal the vast depth and richness of Chinese calligraphic art.
The painting section is divided into three main categories: figure painting, landscape painting, and bird-and-flower painting. Tang dynasty figure paintings feature flowing lines and vivid colors; Song dynasty landscape paintings evoke distant, profound atmospheres with exquisite brushwork; Yuan dynasty literati painting emphasizes expressive freedom, pursuing the ideal of 'effortless and spontaneous brushwork'; Ming and Qing dynasty bird-and-flower paintings combine meticulous detail with expressive spontaneity, each with distinct characteristics. Works by masters such as Gu Kaizhi, Wu Daozi, Fan Kuan, Guo Xi, Zhao Mengfu, Tang Yin, Xu Wei, the Eight Eccentrics of Yangzhou, and Zheng Banqiao showcase the unique charm of Chinese painting.

💎 Jade Gallery (Warm and Lustrous Like Jade, the Virtue of the Gentleman)

The Jade Gallery houses over 400 Chinese jade artifacts from the Neolithic period to the Qing dynasty, illustrating the development of Chinese jade culture. The Chinese have long cherished and revered jade as a symbol of virtue—'the gentleman compares his virtue to jade'—making jade not merely ornamental but also a carrier of culture and spirit.
Exhibits include ritual objects, ornaments, vessels, and display pieces. Neolithic jade artifacts are simple in form, primarily serving as ritual items and tools; Shang and Zhou dynasty jade features intricate patterns, mostly used in sacrifices and rituals; Han dynasty jade exhibits exquisite craftsmanship, with numerous burial jade suits and bi discs; Ming and Qing dynasty jade reached the peak of craftsmanship, with diverse carving techniques and rich themes, ranging from large mountain-shaped display pieces to delicate pendants and ornaments.
Of special note are works made from Hetian jade and jadeite—precious stones with warm, lustrous texture, pure color, and superb carving, representing the finest in jade art. The gallery also introduces jade appraisal knowledge, including the six-character principle: 'color, transparency, uniformity, shape, sound, and light,' enabling visitors to learn how to appreciate the beauty of jade.

The architecture of the Shanghai Museum itself is a work of art worthy of close appreciation. Restaurant Background
Lao Zhengxing Restaurant was established during the Tongzhi reign of the Qing Dynasty (1862), making it one of Shanghai’s oldest Huaiyang cuisine restaurants, with over 160 years of history and regarded as a 'living fossil' in Shanghai’s culinary scene. Founded by Cai Renchang from Ningbo, Zhejiang, it was originally named 'Zhengxing Guan' and later renamed 'Lao Zhengxing', symbolizing 'orthodox prosperity'. After more than a century of development, Lao Zhengxing has become the representative brand of Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine and has been recognized as a 'Chinese Time-Honored Brand'.
The history of Lao Zhengxing is the history of the development of Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine. After Shanghai opened as a treaty port in the late Qing and early Republican era, it became the largest metropolis in the Far East, attracting immigrants from all over who brought diverse food cultures. The founder of Lao Zhengxing combined Ningbo culinary techniques with local Shanghai ingredients, absorbing the strengths of Suzhou, Anhui, and Cantonese cuisines, gradually forming a unique 'Huaiyang' style. Huaiyang cuisine is characterized by 'rich oil and red braising, balanced saltiness, original flavor, and mellow deliciousness' — preserving the freshness of Jiangnan cuisine while adding a rich soy aroma, creating a distinctive Hai Pai (Shanghai-style) flavor.
Lao Zhengxing has witnessed Shanghai’s century-long transformation and has hosted countless celebrities and scholars. Literary figures such as Lu Xun, Guo Moruo, and Mei Lanfang were regular patrons. The restaurant displays many historical photos and artifacts that record its glorious history. Today, while maintaining tradition, Lao Zhengxing continues to innovate, reimagining classic dishes to give the time-honored brand new vitality.
The restaurant’s interior retains the old Shanghai style: rosewood furniture, carved lattice windows, blue-and-white porcelain, and palace lanterns exude an ancient and elegant atmosphere. Plaques and couplets inscribed by renowned calligraphers hang on the walls, reflecting deep cultural heritage. Staff wear traditional Chinese attire and provide attentive, meticulous service, allowing diners to experience the hospitality of a time-honored establishment.
Recommended Dishes
🦐 Stir-Fried Shrimp (Huaiyang Signature Dish, Fragrant and Crispy)
Stir-fried shrimp is one of Lao Zhengxing’s signature dishes and one of the most representative shrimp dishes in Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine. It uses fresh river shrimp (green shrimp), which grow in freshwater lakes such as Taihu and Yangcheng Lake. Though small in size, the meat is exceptionally sweet and tender, lacking the fishy odor of sea shrimp, making it ideal for stir-fried shrimp.
The shrimp must be live; when preparing, trim off the whiskers and legs, keeping the body intact. Rinse repeatedly with clean water and drain thoroughly — this step is crucial, as excess moisture affects the stir-frying effect. After preparation, marinate the shrimp briefly with cooking wine, salt, and white pepper to remove fishiness and enhance flavor.
The key technique of 'stir-frying in hot oil' is: heat the pan until it smokes, oil temperature should reach about 80% heat (approx. 200°C). When the shrimp hit the pan, they should make a 'sizzling' sound — this is the 'burst' effect. Quickly stir-fry to ensure even heating; the shrimp shells turn red and crisp rapidly under high heat, while the meat remains tender and juicy. Add soy sauce, yellow wine, white sugar, and vinegar for seasoning, then stir-fry over high heat to allow the flavors to fully penetrate the meat. The entire process must not exceed 2 minutes to preserve the shrimp’s tenderness and crispiness.
The finished stir-fried shrimp is bright red and glossy, emitting a rich aroma of soy and shrimp. The shells are crisp and crumble easily with a light bite — edible shell included, providing calcium and deliciousness; the meat is tender, juicy, Q-elastic and refreshing, with a hint of sweetness. The sauce is balanced in salt and freshness, with a harmonious sweet-and-sour profile, especially appetizing. This dish is a favorite among Shanghainese as a drinking snack — pair it with a glass of yellow wine or beer, eat and chat, and it’s the perfect enjoyment of a summer evening.
🐍 Sizzling Eel Strips (Time-Honored Classic, Passed Down Generations)
Sizzling eel strips are a traditional signature dish of Lao Zhengxing with over a century of history, and the craftsmanship has been passed down through generations. Lao Zhengxing’s version enjoys great fame in Shanghai, and many old Shanghai residents come specifically to taste this classic dish.
The eel used at Lao Zhengxing is specially selected: it must be wild yellow eel from Taihu or Yangcheng Lake, which grows in clear waters, yielding exceptionally tender and flavorful meat. Preparing the yellow eel requires skilled knife work: remove bones, internal organs, blood, and mucus, leaving only pure eel meat, then cut into uniform, evenly sized strips — a skill requiring years of experience.
The key to stir-frying lies in oil temperature control — Lao Zhengxing’s secret: oil must reach about 90% heat. When the eel strips hit the pan, they instantly set; quickly stir-fry, then add the special sauce (soy sauce, yellow wine, white sugar, vinegar, ginger juice, etc., mixed in secret proportions), and the process flows smoothly in one continuous motion. After cooking, plate the dish, sprinkle generously with scallion and ginger strips, then pour scorching hot oil over it — a loud 'sizzle' erupts, and fragrance explodes instantly — this is the signature moment of Lao Zhengxing’s sizzling eel strips.
When tasted, the eel strips are tender, smooth, and melt in the mouth, with no fishy taste — only pure freshness. Lao Zhengxing’s eel strips are special due to the sauce’s balance: savory and fresh with a hint of sweet-sour, rich in layers yet harmonious — this is the century-old shop’s exclusive secret. Served with a bowl of white rice, the eel strips’ juices mixed with rice are incredibly delicious — the most authentic taste in the memory of old Shanghai residents.
🥬 Chrysanthemum Greens Rings (Time-Honored Signature, Herbaceous and Meaty)
Chrysanthemum greens rings are another signature dish of Lao Zhengxing and one of the most distinctive dishes in Shanghai Huaiyang cuisine. The dish’s history dates back to the late Qing Dynasty, making it one of the restaurant’s original dishes, and it has retained its traditional preparation method to this day.
Lao Zhengxing's grass head circle uses exquisite ingredients; pork large intestine must be selected fresh, repeatedly washed at least 10 times to remove all odors, then marinated with cooking wine, ginger juice, and salt, followed by blanching to remove fishiness. The cleaned large intestine is cut into rings, placed in the pot for braising, with Lao Zhengxing's secret sauce added, and simmered over low heat until tender and flavorful, a process requiring at least 2 hours.
Grass heads should be selected from the tenderest alfalfa sprouts in spring, washed clean, then quickly stir-fried over high heat, adding salt, minced garlic, and yellow wine for seasoning, stir-fried until just cooked through to maintain the grass head's vibrant green and fresh aroma. Stir-frying time must not exceed 1 minute; if too long, the grass heads will turn yellow and tough, losing their fresh fragrance.
Finally, mix the braised pork intestine rings with the stir-fried grass heads, quickly stir-fry to allow the grass head's fresh aroma to penetrate the intestine, while the intestine's richness envelops the grass head. The brilliance of this dish lies in the balance of meat and vegetable, richness and lightness: the pork intestine is soft, glutinous, and rich, carrying the salty freshness of thick oil and red sauce along with the fragrance of spices; the grass head is refreshing, crisp, and tender, carrying plant fragrance and a hint of bitterness. Together, they cut through the intestine's greasiness while enhancing the grass head's freshness, resulting in rich texture layers and balanced nutrition.
🍲 Xian Du Xian (Spring seasonal, salty-fresh and rich)
Xian Du Xian is a traditional famous dish from the Jiangnan region and a必备 soup on Shanghai spring tables. 'Xian' refers to cured meat, 'Du' to fresh pork, and 'Xian' in Shanghai dialect means slow simmering over low heat. This dish combines the salty fragrance of cured meat with the fresh sweetness of fresh pork, plus the清香 of spring bamboo shoots, forming a unique compound freshness.
Lao Zhengxing's Xian Du Xian pays special attention to ingredient selection: the cured meat must be homemade, cured for at least one month to allow full flavor penetration; the fresh meat should be pork belly or ribs, with tender texture; spring bamboo shoots should be selected from around Qingming Festival, when they are most tender and free of bitterness. Additional ingredients include winter mushrooms, tofu skin knots, and tofu puffs, enhancing texture and nutrition.
When stewing, first place cured and fresh meat in cold water, bring to a boil over high heat, skim off foam, then reduce to low heat for slow simmering. Simmering time must be at least 2 hours to make the meat tender and the soup milky white. Spring bamboo shoots are cut into roll-cut chunks and added 30 minutes before the soup finishes, allowing them to absorb the soup's freshness while infusing their own fragrance into the broth. Finally, add winter mushrooms, tofu skin knots, etc., and simmer for another 10 minutes.
The served Xian Du Xian has a milky white broth and fragrant aroma. One spoonful reveals layered flavors: the salty fragrance of cured meat, the fresh sweetness of fresh meat, the清香 of spring bamboo shoots, and the mushroom香 of winter mushrooms—rich in freshness yet not greasy. The cured meat is tender and flavorful, the fresh meat soft and not dry, the spring bamboo shoots crisp and refreshing; each ingredient retains its character while harmonizing into a unified whole. This soup is especially good with rice—mixing broth with rice yields exquisite freshness, making it the most nourishing spring delicacy.
